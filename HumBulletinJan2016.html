Small-scale farmers bear the brunt of El Niño in southern Mindanao

Breadbasket of Mindanao hit by prolonged dry spell

While the rains from Typhoon Melor (known locally as Nona), Tropical Depression Onyok and the enhanced northeast monsoon brought much-needed respite from the drying effects of El Niño, large areas of the Philippines are expected to suffer droughts in the coming months. Small-scale farmers of southern Mindanao are already suffering a big drop in their crop production, and fear that El Niño might push them into a cycle of debt over three to four farming seasons.

Region XII of the Philippines, composed of South Cotabato, North Cotabato, Sultan Kudarat and Sarangani provinces, is considered the breadbasket of Mindanao. The region supplies over 40 per cent of the national food requirements and contributes more than 30 per cent of food export according to FAO. Farmers here produce staple crops including rice and corn and cultivate large plantations of cash crops such as bananas and pineapples.

The prevailing El Niño has dried up the region in the past year. Sarangani province in particular recorded below-normal (40-80 per cent of average) rainfall for seven months and way-below-normal (less than 40 per cent of average) rainfall for three months in 2015. As a result, farmers in Sarangani received only 54 per cent of annual rainfall last year, according to the Philippine Atmospheric Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA).
Alabel, Sarangani province, 8 January 2016. Jennie Korbo stands by her withering crop of corn. Credit: OCHA/ M. Malang

Landless farmers without coping mechanisms suffer production loss

For Jennie Korbo, a small-scale farmer in Alabel municipality of Sarangai, waiting for the rain was not an option. She grows corn on her rented 1.8-hectare land, which yields 3.7 tons or 148 sacks of corn during a normal farming season. Due to frequent water shortage this season, her corn has been withered and she is expecting to harvest less than 1 ton or about 40 sacks.

Though she had heard about El Niño and its effects on the radio, she took the risk of planting in August 2015 because she could not afford to delay harvest. In addition to PhP7,000 (US$147) annual rent for the farmland, Jennie had borrowed P30,000 ($628) to buy seeds, fertilizer and chemical spray for the corn. With most of her corn damaged, she lost over P10,000 ($209) net profit this season and can pay back only P8,000 ($167) out of her loans. A working mother of five, Jennie is worried her family might remain in debt for several farming seasons to come.

Many landless farmers in Mindanao share similar stories. While they cultivate one of the richest farmlands in the country, they are often chronically food insecure. Bad harvest can strain their access to food; they can neither consume their own production nor make sufficient income to purchase food. Coupled with low literacy level and dependence on rural livelihoods, they find it extremely difficult to cope with the impact of El Niño.

The Government steps up mitigation measures

The Department of Agriculture (DA) through its regional office has carried out various measures to mitigate the impact of El Niño to support the farmers like Jennie. These include distribution of open source pumps; construction and rehabilitation of small impounding projects, diversion dams, and irrigation canals; pest surveillance; cloud seeding; and distribution of replacement seeds and drought-tolerant and early-maturing crop varieties to affected farmers. The DA also engaged in information and education campaigns to raise awareness on how to cope with El Niño through conducting training on soil and water conservation and climate change adaptation to shift cropping calendars.
The provincial and municipal authorities of Sarangani and Alabel are also assisting affected farmers through lending tractors to rehabilitate farmlands, increasing the production in areas not affected by El Niño to fill the gaps left by crop damage and purchasing additional rice and corn seeds.

About 85 per cent of the country to experience drought by April

The PAGASA predicts that El Niño will remain strong until February and most provinces in central and western Mindanao will continue to experience below-average rainfall from January to April. By the end of April, 85 per cent of the country including the whole of Region XII will face drought. The DA is carrying out an impact assessment on food security with production data to be compiled by March. 

Persisting dry conditions and resulting water shortage may also have health implications. Local health station in Jennie’s neighbourhood reported increased incidence of diarrhoea, cold-like symptoms and hypertension over the recent months. Skin disease is also on the rise. The health workers fear that El Niño may worsen the situation and increase malnutrition among children.

Triple weather disturbances hit the Philippines

Over 700,000 people pre-emptively evacuate ahead of typhoon landfall

Amid the response to Typhoon Koppu (known locally as Lando), which battered central and northern Luzon in October, three more weather disturbances affected the Philippines in December.

From 14 to 16 December, Typhoon Melor carved through central Philippines carrying destructive winds of 150 km/h and gusts of up to 185 km/h, and heavy rainfall. About 743,000 people along the typhoon’s projected path pre-emptively evacuated to safer grounds. Typhoon Melor made five landfalls in the provinces of Northern Samar, Sorsogon, Masbate, Romblon and Oriental Mindoro, leaving a trail of destruction which resulted in 42 deaths, over 270,000 damaged houses and PhP6.45 billion ($135 million) damages in agriculture and infrastructure.

Authorities were still assessing the impact of Typhoon Melor when a tropical depression known locally as Onyok made landfall over Davao Oriental province in Mindanao on 18 December. Tropical Depression Onyok dumped heavy rains over Mindanao Island, providing relief to parched lands that suffered from the effects of El Niño. The tropical depression also enhanced the Northeast Monsoon, which brought rains over the eastern parts of Luzon, Metro Manila and the Bicol Region and further hampered the recovery in areas affected by Typhoons Koppu and Melor.

State of national calamity declared to facilitate localised response

The President of the Philippines on 18 December declared a state of national calamity to allow local authorities to access funds from the national budget to hasten the rescue, relief, recovery, and rehabilitation efforts following the three hydro-meteorological hazards.

The Government’s response to the successive weather events was indicative of its efforts to devolve operational leadership for preparedness and response from the national to regional and local authorities. This was supported by the Government’s early warning system that provided timely and improved hazard information that facilitated local actions.

Humanitarian Country Team provides targeted assistance

The members of the Humanitarian Country Team (HCT) and its partners provided emergency shelter, food and non-food items, logistics, water, sanitation and hygiene, health, early recovery, livelihood, and community engagement support as requested by government departments and affected local authorities, and as assessments revealed the need. This was done in a manner similar to the response to Typhoon Koppu – with assistance mobilised primarily with resources available in the country – following the Government’s preference.

To elucidate lessons of the response to these recent weather disturbances, the HCT proposed an after action review with the Government in early 2016. In particular, many humanitarian agencies in the Philippines relied on relief items stocked from previous emergency operations to response to the Government’s request for assistance with in-country resources. These supplies however are finite and need to be urgently replenished ahead of another disaster.
In parallel to the discussion on international resource mobilisation, the HCT will continue to support the Government to enhance coordination of joint rapid needs assessment, aided by appropriate technologies and procedures for effective information gathering, sharing and analysis.

Way forward for Zamboanga IDPs

Over 24,600 people remain displaced without durable solutions

2015 was another long year for over 24,600 internally displaced people (IDPs) who lost their homes to the September 2013 conflict in Zamboanga City. The Government’s roadmap to provide sustainable housing solutions for the displaced families has been delayed considerably beyond its original implementation target of June 2015.
Meanwhile, humanitarian agencies have stretched their resources to fill the gaps in basic services for the IDPs who have been without sustainable livelihoods for over two years. They also advocated crucial protection needs of the IDPs, many of whom are of minority Muslim groups with indigenous origin, to be adequately informed of the Government’s housing plan and to negotiate assistance for their socially- and culturally-specific needs.

As of 4 January, some 16,900 IDPs are staying in 12 transitional sites across the city, waiting for a new home where they can rebuild their lives. With little progress in the construction of permanent housing, most of these IDPs are expected to remain in the transitional sites well past March. In addition, an estimated 7,700 “home-based” IDPs are continually hosted by relatives and friends or renting temporary homes.

In September 2013, violent fighting erupted in Zamboanga City between the Government forces and a faction of the Moro National Liberation Front. The three-week conflict left over 10,000 houses destroyed and about 118,000 people displaced.

Zambaonga City, 10 December 2015. UN Resident and Humanitarian Coordinator Ola Almgren visits displaced families and camp managers of the city’s largest IDP transitional site in Mampang. Credit: OCHA/A. Hashim

Visit of the Humanitarian Coordinator and Way Forward workshop

Mr. Ola Almgren, the UN Resident and Humanitarian Coordinator in the Philippines, visited Zamboanga City from 10 to 11 December. Mr. Almgren visited transitional sites in Mampang and Kasanyangan, where he spoke with camp managers and displaced families who shared their concerns over the lack of water and livelihoods. He also visited the permanent housing site in Mariki and met with the city mayor to discuss recovery needs in Zamboanga. At the monthly meeting of the Zamboanga Mindanao Humanitarian Team (ZMHT), Mr. Almgren acknowledged the challenges surrounding slow transition to early recovery and stressed the HCT’s commitment to maintaining its focus on Mindanao including Zamboanga in 2016.

On 17 December, ZMHT members invited sectoral representatives of the city authorities to discuss key residual concerns for the IDPs in Zamboanga and ways forward in recovery. CFSI, CRS, FAO, ILO, IOM and UNHCR confirmed to maintain its field presence in Zambaonga into 2016. These agencies will continually support the city authorities with provision of temporary and permanent shelter, sustainable livelihood, camp management and protection assistance to the remaining IDPs.

Water shortage feared to escalate the tension between IDPs and their host communities

Zamboanga City re-introduced periodic water rationing on 8 January as the water level of a local dam reached a critical level due to persisting dry spell. On 12 January, the city authorities declared a state of calamity to allow implementation of contingency plans using the Government’s calamity fund. While the IDPs at the transitional sites were planned to be priorities for water delivery, insufficient water supply is reportedly escalating the tension between the displaced families and their host communities. An expected drought in the coming months may put further pressure to the IDPs.

With slow progress in the Government’s housing plan, tens of thousands of IDPs may have to stay in transitional sites beyond March.

In brief

Zamboanga community engagement learning review report released

The report of Zamboanga Learning Review on Post-Conflict Community Engagement was released on 14 January. The report captures good practices and lessons of the community engagement efforts spearheaded by the Zamboanga Communications Working Group. The group was established in October 2013 under the leadership of the City’s Public Information Office and was joined by international and local aid agencies to facilitate humanitarian communications and accountability of aid for the displaced families. The report underscores the importance of mainstreaming two-way communication, community participation and common service partnerships in all emergency response programming including in post-conflict settings.
